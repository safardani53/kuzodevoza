Fake BT email warning as scammers target your cash with phony bills sent out to customers

 Posted on May 30, 2024  |  3 minutes  |  515 words  |  Aldo Pusey

FRAUDSTERS are sending BT customers extortionate bill amounts in a new email scam in the hope of panicking them into clicking on a dodgy link.

BT is investigating the new phishing scam in which customers are told BT will "take your payment from your account as usual by Direct Debit".

One social media user shared a screengrab of the bogus bill which said the customer was being charged £996.63.

Cllr Warren Whyte tweeted a picture of the scam email which urges victims to click through to view their bill.

It reads: "This doesn't include any amounts brought forward from any other bills.

"We've put your latest BT bill for you to view.

"The PDF version of your bill might not be available for download yet. It can take up to 48 hours."

The email looks particularly legitimate as it has a BT letterhead saying "Keeping you informed" and includes info about switching to paperless bills.

It claims to be signed by CEO of BT Business "Erin Rose", but the real CEO is called Graham Sutherland.

BT said on Twitter: "We are currently investigating this issue. Thanks for highlighting it."

A spokesman said: "Some e-mails come with malicious attachments claiming to be from BT or asking them to follow links to fake sites and enter their details.

"These emails aren’t actually from us. They’re from scammers, but are designed to look like they’re from us so you trust them and do what they ask."

He urged anyone sent the bogus emails not to click on any links and forward them to phishing@bt.com.

The spokesman said: "BT takes customer security very seriously and will never ask for their personal information out of the blue or call from an ‘unknown’ number.

"If we’re getting in touch about their bill, it will either be from 0800 328 9393 or 0800 028 5085.
"Our team of security experts are working around the clock to block these scams and related websites as fast as possible.

"We are working to introduce new systems and standards such as DMARC to prevent these scams taking place in the future."

The scam is similar to one targeting Vodafone and O2 customers, encouraging them to click on a link which downloads a virus that can steal your bank details.

The emails look official at first glance, but on closer inspection there are typos, grammatical errors and bad English, which would never be in an official email.

The links in the emails likely contain Emotet, a type of malware that could steal bank account details by intercepting traffic.

It comes after a mum-to-be revealed she had £65,000 stolen from her account after being tricked into giving fraudsters a passcode over the phone.

Claire Pearson received what appeared to be a legitimate text from Santander, but it was actually a scam.

Text-based scams - known as "smishing", short for SMS phishing - have also been targeting NHS patients, asking for personal details.
