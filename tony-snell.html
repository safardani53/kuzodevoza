Tony Snell | Early Life, Family, & Education

Snell was born on November 10th, 1991, in Watts, California. Single mother, Sherika Brown, raised him and his sister Tonyecia Snell.

His mother worked hard to support the Snell siblings. Tony grew up being inspired by Micheal Jordan and would sleep with his Little Tikes basketball.

Snell copied Jordan’s moves and practiced passionately. As Tony had dark skin and a headband, just like Jordan, people often called him Baby Jordan.

He picked his actions fast and started becoming a standout player by the age of seven. Older kids noticed Tony for his skills, which made him often play with children past his age.

In an interview with the NBA in 2013, Snell disclosed that he has other family members apart from his mother and sister, including his stepfather, another younger sister, and a younger brother. However, their details are not available to date.

According to the horoscope, Snell is a Scorpio. The most common trait in Scorpio is that Scorpios are mostly determined and ambitious.

These traits are noticeable in Snell from his early childhood till his career today.

Education

Tony attended Martin Luther King High School, located in Riverside, California, and during his senior year attended Westwind Preparatory Academy located in Pheonix, Arizona. Later, for his college, he attended the University of New Mexico.

Tony Snell | Career and Profession

High School Career

During his time attending Martin Luther King High School, he and his teammate Kawhi Leonard led the team towards a national ranking of #7 in the MaxPreps/National Guard computer rankings.

Snell’s average under head coach Tim Sweeney was seven rebounds, fourteen points, three assists, and four blocks per game. During his senior year in Westwind, Snell’s average was ten rebounds, 19.5 points, 1.8 blocks, and 8.8 assists.

College Career

According to ESPN’s scouting reports, Snell is considered “a diamond in the rough” and “one of the West Coast’s top sleepers.” For his University, he attended the University of Mexico and played for the college team called “New Mexico Lobos.”

During 2010-2011, Team Lobo was led by lone seniors Dairese Gary and Drew Gordon, a UCLA Transfer.

Tony was one of the freshmen playing consequential minutes with Cameron Bairstow, Kendall Williams, and Alex Kirk. They were one of the most accomplished freshman classes in Lobo.

During the season 2010-2011, the team had a mix of wins and losses. The team received an offer to the 2011 National Invitation Tournament, where they defeated UTEP but eventually lost to Alabama.

Tony led the team in scoring twice by scoring double-figures four times and played in all games but one where his average was two rebounds and 4.4 points.

He had a blow-up performance by hitting sixteen points and four three-pointers while playing against then #9 BYU Cougars and following two games later against Wyoming by season-best 19 with five threes.

During the first seven games, his average was 8.3 points and 10.3 points during the seven-game stretch with 17-31 shots.

Later, due to a sprained ankle, he limited his play and cut his production in the late season. During the last games, he managed to score only eleven points in total.

2011-2012 season

In the season 2011-12, the Mexico Lobos defensive field goal percentage was seventh nationally, and their scoring defense was fourteenth.

After a three-year starter and a senior Phillip McDonald was injured, Tony played as a guard with several young players.

He acquired a starting spot after doing well in practice while also playing exceptionally well in the preseason exhibition games. Snell was capable of critical scoring but was also likely to become indefinite and fall into a slump.

He scored double-digits in twenty games of the season, together with three games over 20 points and six threes within one of the games.

On the other hand, he also scored six or fewer points in eleven games and finishing scoreless for two straight games. His average during the season was 2.7 rebounds, 2.3 assists, and 10.5 points per game.

In the conference tournament’s semi-finals, the Lobos won against #20 UNLV, with Snell scoring fifteen points, six rebounds, and six assists.

They won the championship game against #18 San Diego State, where Snell scored a total of fourteen points, shooting 4-6 from three, and won the title All-Tournament team.

The Lobos received a five-seed rank in the NCAA Tournament. The Lobos finished the season with a score of 28-7, where Snell grabbed the title of Honorable Mention All-Mountain West.

2012–13 season

During the 2012-13 season, Snell’s average was 12.5 points, 2.6 rebounds, and 2.9 assists per game, where he guided the team in three-point shooting percentage for the second season straight.

He was the fifth most exact free throw shooter in Lobo’s history over his college team career. The New Mexico Lobos began the season with 12-0, including a win over #19 Connecticut, ranking on #16.

Due to a bad loss, while playing against #20 San Diego State, the team won nine out of ten games and settled with the MWC regular-season title, also reaching #11.

Snell’s mother termed him as the “silent assassin” due to his quiet demeanor, which became a nickname that his fans embraced.

In the championship game at the University of Nevada, Snell shot 5-7 from three, 8-11 from the field, and 13 straight points for the team leading towards winning their second consecutive tournament title.

Snell’s exceptional performance earned him Tournament MVP and ESPN National Player of the Week. After the season, he got the title Third Team All-MWC. New Mexico Lobos entered the NCAA Tournament and ranked #10.

Professional Career

Tony chose not to attend his senior season for college and participated in the NBA draft.

While his college stats were not striking enough, NBA scouts said that the Lobo’s balanced offense limited his output, but they were impressed by his length, outside shooting, and significantly improved defense during his games.

Chicago Bulls

In the 2012 NBA draft, Snell was selected by the Chicago Bulls as the 20th overall pick.

On July 10th, 2013, he signed with them and joined for the 2013 NBA Summer League, where his average was 6.6 rebounds, 11.8 points, 1.2 steals, and 2.2 assists in a total of five games.

As a rookie, Tony appeared in 77 games, with an average of 1.6 rebounds and 4.5 points.

Snell rejoined the Bulls in July 2014, for the 2014 NBA Summer League, where his average was 20 points, four rebounds, and 2.8 assists in a total of five games, which led him towards All-NBA Summer League first-team honors.

He scored his career-high of points on February 10th, 2015, in the Bulls’ 104–86 win against the Sacramento Kings. Eventually, Snell became the starting small forward for the Bulls to begin the 2015–16 season.

In the first 25 games of the season, Tony started in all but four games. Meanwhile, on December 21st, he was removed by the Bulls from the team’s regular rotation.

His teammate Doug McDermott got injured on December 28th while playing against the Toronto Raptors and Snell received his chance to re-establish himself.

As a result, he scored an equal team-high of twenty-two points on 8-of-14 shooting within 27 minutes in the game, leading towards the defeat of the Raptors by 104–97.

Milwaukee Bucks

Snell was traded to Milwaukee Bucks by the Chicago Bulls on October 17th, 2016, in exchange for Michael Carter-Williams.

Tony made his debut for the Bucks in the second game of the season on October 29th, 2016, where he scored six points in 21 minutes as a starter and a 110–108 win over the Brooklyn Nets.

On December 26th, 2016, Snell made a career-high six three-pointers and set a season-high record with 20 points.

However, the Bucks lost to the Washington Wizards. On March 6th, 2017, he had another season-high of 21 in the first half with a win against the Philadelphia 76ers.

Soon, he surpassed that mark on March 28th with a career-high of twenty-six points in a win against the Charlotte Hornets. He re-signed a four-year, $44 million contract with the Bucks on July 31st, 2017.

Reportedly, the deal included a player option for reachable incentives after the third year, which would push his total earnings to $46 million.

Injuries
